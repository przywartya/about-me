# Ask for Feedback (Don't Just Give It)

Most leadership advice about feedback focuses on how to *give* it better. Better frameworks, better timing, better sandwiches. But the real unlock isn't learning to give feedback better — it's building a culture where your direct reports **ask you for feedback themselves.**

When that happens, 80% of the feedback conversations you used to dread become a walk in the park. Instead of you delivering unsolicited criticism — triggering threat responses, watching someone's walls go up — they're sitting across from you *inviting* you to tell them what to change. The dynamic flips entirely.

This isn't a technique for handling a crisis. If you have a people problem burning right now, go deal with it — I wrote about that in [Every Day You Don't Act, You're Choosing to Live With the Problem](/https://github.com/przywartya/about-me/blob/main/Your%20Team%20Already%20Knows%20You're%20Avoiding%20the%20Conversation.md). 

The article you are reading right now is the long game. This is what you build into your team's culture over months, and the payoff is that the hard conversations mostly stop being hard.

## Why Asking Works Better Than Giving

Research from the NeuroLeadership Institute backs this up: when someone asks for feedback, the threat response drops dramatically — for both sides. The person asking is in control (autonomy). They chose to hear it (certainty). And the person responding isn't in the uncomfortable position of delivering unsolicited criticism.

If you've read about the SCARF model — Status, Certainty, Autonomy, Relatedness, and Fairness — asking for feedback naturally defuses almost every one of those threat domains at once.

But here's what matters in practice: **you go first.** You model the behavior by asking your team for feedback on you. Over time, they start doing the same thing back. And once your direct report is sitting in a 1:1 saying "hey, how do you think I handled that project?" — you're no longer the person delivering uncomfortable news. You're answering a question they asked. That's a fundamentally different conversation.

## How to Do It

As a leader, you model this. You are asking your team all the time: what can I do better? What's not working? How am I handling this situation?

But here's the key: **you have to make it personal.** Don't ask "how's the team doing?" That's too abstract. Ask "how am I dealing with this issue?" or "are you happy with how I've been solving this problem so far?" Give them a specific thing to react to.

And get specific about your own growth edges. If you're not sure whether you're a good active listener, ask: "Do you think I'm a good active listener? I honestly don't know, and I'd like your honest take." That kind of vulnerability — admitting you don't know something about yourself — is what opens the door to real feedback.

## People Will Lie to You (At First)

Here's the tricky part — people will lie to you. They will say everything is fine and you're doing great, because you're their boss. You have power over their career. So the first few times you ask, you'll get polished, safe answers.

That's normal. Keep asking.

The trust builds over time. And when you finally break through — when someone gives you real, honest feedback for the first time — be happy about it. Give yourself a pat on the back. You just broke out of a negative cycle.

## Teach Your Team to Ask Too

This isn't just a leader thing. Once you've modeled the behavior, coach your team to do the same with each other. When your team members are comfortable asking their peers "hey, how did that PR review land? Was I too blunt?" — that's when you've built a feedback culture that sustains itself.

The goal is a team where feedback flows naturally in all directions, not just downhill from the manager. When that culture exists, the hard conversations — the ones where you *have* to give direct feedback about a behavior change — become significantly less threatening, because everyone is already used to hearing honest input.

## This Is Infrastructure, Not Incident Response

I want to be explicit about this: building a feedback-asking culture takes months, not days. It's not something you introduce in the middle of a performance crisis. If you're already tracking someone on a spreadsheet and heading toward a PIP, you missed this window — for now.

But start today anyway. Not for the current fire. For the next one. And the one after that.

Because when you get there — when asking for feedback is just how your team operates — you'll realize that most feedback conversations have stopped being difficult. Your reports are coming to you and asking what they can do better. You're not ambushing anyone. You're not rehearsing how to frame bad news. You're just answering honestly, because they asked.

That's the 80% that becomes easy. The remaining 20% — the serious performance issues, the PIPs, the exits — will still be hard. But you'll have the trust in the room to handle them.

**The best time to start asking for feedback was six months ago. The second best time is right now.**
