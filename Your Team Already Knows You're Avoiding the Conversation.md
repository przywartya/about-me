# Every Day You Don't Act, You're Choosing to Live With the Problem

There's a moment every engineering leader hits where someone on your team brings you a problem about a teammate. Maybe it's a Slack message that's impossible to parse. Maybe it's sloppy code that the rest of the team keeps cleaning up after. Maybe it's a pattern of missing the mark that's been going on for weeks.

And you think: "I'll keep an eye on it."

That's the beginning of the end. Not for the person with the problem — for you and your team. Because every day you're not solving a problem, you are choosing to live with it. And worse, you're choosing that state for your team too.

## You Can't Ignore It

When you see something that needs to change as a leader, you can't pretend it's not there. When your team brings a problem to you, it's your obligation to address it. Not "think about it." Not "monitor it." Address it.

If you see a problem yourself, and your team has also brought it to you, and you're still not acting on it — then you are actively choosing a state for your team where they have to live with that problem. You're telling them, through your inaction, that this is acceptable. That their frustration doesn't matter enough for you to do something uncomfortable.

And make no mistake — it is uncomfortable. Feedback is one of the hardest parts of leadership. But that's the job.

## What Feedback Actually Is

One of the first things I tell founders and newer leaders when they come to me with people problems: **feedback is a tool to get a specific behavior change.** That's it. Not a general feeling that something is off. Not a vague "they need to communicate better." A concrete behavior that needs to stop — or start :)

The problem I see over and over is that leaders know something needs to change, but they make the conversation so soft that the person walks away not understanding the message. They do the shit sandwich. They hedge. They say "maybe you could try..." when what they mean is "this needs to change." The feedback gets buried under so much politeness that the actual point is lost — and then the leader is frustrated that nothing changed, when in reality they never clearly said what needed to change in the first place.

Everything that follows — the techniques, the frameworks, the timing — is in service of that one thing: being direct and clear about a behavior change you need.

## The Brain Science: Why Feedback Goes Wrong

Here's why feedback is so hard: our brains treat social threats the same way they treat physical ones. When someone hears unsolicited criticism, their brain activates a fight-or-flight response. Cognitive resources shut down right when the person needs them most. They stop learning and start defending.

David Rock's SCARF model breaks this down into five domains that trigger these threat responses: **Status, Certainty, Autonomy, Relatedness, and Fairness.** Understanding these five things will fundamentally change how you deliver feedback — and how you ask for it.

**Status** is about perceived standing. This is where leaders accidentally fall into the shit sandwich they think they're avoiding. The instinct is to lead with praise to soften the blow — but that's not what protecting status means.
Protecting status means making sure the person doesn't hear "you're bad at your job" when what you're actually saying is "this specific thing needs to change." It's about scope. If you walk into a conversation and your framing makes it feel like a referendum on their worth, their brain shuts down before you get to the point. But if it's clear you're talking about one behavior — not who they are — the threat stays manageable.
The difference is subtle but it matters: you're not buttering them up before the bad news. You're making sure the conversation stays about the behavior, not their identity. And never, ever compare them negatively to someone else on the team. That's a status nuke — it tells them their standing relative to their peers is at risk, and now they're not thinking about the feedback at all. They're thinking about survival.

**Certainty** is about predictability. Vague feedback like "you need to communicate better" creates anxiety because the person has no idea what to do with it. Be specific. Give actionable steps. Break improvement areas into manageable chunks so the path forward is clear, not foggy.

**Autonomy** is about control. If you dictate every step of someone's improvement plan, you strip away their sense of ownership. Instead, involve them. Let them self-reflect. Ask them how they think they could improve. When people draw their own conclusions, they're far more invested in following through.

**Relatedness** is about connection and safety. This is the one that takes time. When I tell someone "it took me a while to build this relationship with you" — that's relatedness. It's me being honest about the fact that trust doesn't happen overnight, and that the effort was worth it. Feedback delivered without any relational foundation feels cold and threatening. Feedback delivered inside a trusting relationship feels like care.

**Fairness** is about consistency. If you only give feedback when things go wrong, or if different people get different treatment for the same behavior, you trigger a fairness threat. Use objective data. Be consistent. Don't surprise people at the end of a cycle with feedback they never heard during the year.

## A Note on "Ask for Feedback"

There's a practice I believe is foundational for making all of this easier: **building a culture where your direct reports ask you for feedback themselves.** When that happens, 80% of the feedback conversations you used to dread become a walk in the park — because they're inviting you to tell them what to change, not the other way around. I wrote about how to build this in a separate post: [Ask for Feedback (Don't Just Give It)](/ask-for-feedback).

But I want to be clear about where it fits. Asking for feedback is a **long-term, preventive strategy** — it's something you build into your team's culture over months. It is not a tool for putting out a fire. If you're reading this article because you have a problem right now and you never built that foundation, don't stop to introduce "ask for feedback" into your team before addressing the issue. Solve the problem first. Start building the culture in parallel, so next time it's easier.

## The "I Statement" Technique

When it's time to actually deliver feedback — whether you're the leader or you're coaching a team member on how to give feedback to a peer — use "I statements." The format is simple:

**"When you [specific behavior], I [impact on me]."**

Not "you're a bad communicator." Not "your messages are confusing." Instead: "When you send messages without context about the feature you're referencing, I have to spend extra time figuring out what you mean, and it slows down the whole team."

This does two things. First, it keeps the conversation about behavior, not character. You're not attacking who they are — you're describing what they did and how it affected you. Second, it gives them something concrete to change. They know exactly what behavior to adjust.

And here's the part most people miss: **deliver it in the moment.** If someone interrupts you in a meeting, don't wait two weeks to bring it up in a 1:1. Stop and say it right there: "Hey, when you interrupt me, I lose my train of thought and it's hard for me to make my point." The closer the feedback is to the behavior, the more effective it is.

## Building Trust Through Hard Conversations

Here's the paradox that most people don't expect: **going through a really hard feedback conversation actually builds trust.** Not destroys it — builds it.

When you sit down with someone and have an honest, difficult conversation — and you come out the other side — you've proven that the relationship can handle hard things. That's the foundation of real trust. It's not the easy conversations that build trust. It's the hard ones.

And this applies in both directions. If you're a team member who just had a tough conversation with your manager and they actually listened — give that positive feedback back. Say "thank you for listening to me. I felt like this was a lost cause, and now that I know you'll hear me out, I feel more comfortable bringing things up in the future." Or after the next meeting where you notice they've changed: "Hey, I noticed that in that meeting you were listening carefully and asking good questions." Reinforce the behavior you want to see. Feedback goes both ways.

## Check Your Bias

When you're in the middle of a people problem, it's easy to develop confirmation bias. You decide someone isn't good at something, and then everything they do looks like evidence that you're right. You stop seeing the good. You stop being neutral.

**You have to constantly fight to stay neutral.** Especially as a leader, you are not a peer in this relationship. You have power. If you decide someone can't change before you've given them a real chance, you've already failed them. Keep the spreadsheet (more on that below). Let the data speak. Don't let your narrative run ahead of the facts.

And sometimes — this is the Bruce Wang advice — **you have to give your manager a break.** They're going through a lot too. That person you're frustrated with? They might be dealing with things you don't know about. This doesn't excuse bad behavior, but it should inform your approach. Assume good intent until the data tells you otherwise.

## Getting Your Team to Tell You the Truth

Trust doesn't show up on day one. Your team doesn't trust you yet when you start. They don't know if you'll use their honesty against them. So you build trust by working together and not breaking it when working together. It's an organic process. Trust grows when you act coherently — when what you say and what you do are the same thing.

Obviously, if you're lying to your direct reports, there is no basis for trust and it will not grow. You can't ask for honesty while being dishonest yourself.

And when you finally break through — when someone gives you real, honest feedback for the first time — be happy about it. Give yourself a pat on the back. You just broke out of a negative cycle. **Trust with your manager is foundational**, and the same is true in reverse: trust between you and your reports is the thing everything else is built on.

## When Feedback Isn't Enough: The PIP

Sometimes you give feedback, you give space, you give time, and things don't change. That's when you need to get structured about it.

**Write everything down.** Make a spreadsheet. Create a cell for each day. Document occurrences — both the negative ones and the positive ones. You want a factual record, not a feeling.

**Give a timeframe for change.** I've given different time slots depending on the situation — sometimes a month, sometimes a quarter. It depends on how severe the problem is, how much you value the person, and how much they're contributing in other areas. The better they are at other things, the harder the final decision is.

**It's a game of balance.** Are you happy to live with the negative consequences of this problem continuing to exist on your team? That's the question you keep coming back to. The spreadsheet helps you answer it with evidence rather than gut feel.

**Track your team's feedback too.** It's the team who is suffering the most from a teammate's underperformance. They're the ones cleaning up the mess, decoding the unclear messages, picking up the slack. Their feedback goes into the spreadsheet too. Their experience matters.

## The Decision

At some point, you'll have enough data to make a call. Maybe the person turned it around — great, the spreadsheet will show that. Maybe they didn't.

**Once you've made the decision to let someone go, make it quick.** The longer you keep someone around who no longer lives in trust and truth within the team, the worse it gets for morale. You will start acting weird — you're a human, and this is an emotional moment. Your team will notice. The energy shifts. Conversations become guarded. The uncertainty is corrosive.

Every day you delay after making the decision is another day you're choosing to let the team suffer for it.

## New Hires vs. Veterans

There's a fallacy here. People think it's easier to let go of new hires than veterans. In practice, with new hires you're constantly giving them the benefit of the doubt. "They're still ramping up." "Let's wait and see." And before you know it, months have passed and you've been observing instead of acting.

The spreadsheet helps with this too. It gives you an honest record of what's actually happening versus what you're hoping will happen.

---

**Every day you don't solve a problem, you're choosing to live with that problem.** That applies to code, to architecture, to product decisions — but most of all, it applies to people. The people problems are the ones leaders avoid the longest, and they're the ones that do the most damage when left unresolved.
